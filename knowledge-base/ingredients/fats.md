# Cooking Fats

## Overview

Fat is a fundamental cooking medium that serves as a heat-transfer agent, a flavor carrier, a texturizer, and a source of mouthfeel. Selecting the correct fat for a given application requires understanding smoke points, flavor profiles, fatty acid composition, and how each behaves under heat. Professional kitchens maintain a battery of fats for different purposes.

## Smoke Points and Applications

The smoke point is the temperature at which a fat begins to break down, producing acrolein (a harsh, acrid compound) and visible smoke. Exceeding the smoke point degrades flavor and produces harmful compounds. Refined fats have higher smoke points than unrefined due to the removal of free fatty acids and particulate matter.

| Fat | Smoke Point | Flavor Profile | Best Applications |
|---|---|---|---|
| Refined avocado oil | 520°F (271°C) | Neutral | High-heat searing, deep frying, grilling |
| Refined safflower oil | 510°F (266°C) | Neutral | Deep frying, high-heat saute |
| Rice bran oil | 490°F (254°C) | Mild, clean | Stir-fry, tempura, deep frying |
| Refined peanut oil | 450°F (232°C) | Mildly nutty | Deep frying, Asian wok cookery |
| Ghee / clarified butter | 450°F (232°C) | Rich, nutty, dairy | Searing, Indian cookery, sauteing |
| Refined canola oil | 400°F (204°C) | Neutral | All-purpose, baking, frying |
| Grapeseed oil | 390°F (199°C) | Very neutral | Vinaigrettes, medium-heat saute |
| Lard (rendered leaf lard) | 370°F (188°C) | Clean, porky | Pastry, frying, confit |
| Duck fat | 375°F (191°C) | Rich, savory | Confit, roasting potatoes, sauteing |
| Whole unsalted butter | 350°F (177°C) | Sweet, dairy, complex | Finishing, baking, gentle saute |
| Unrefined coconut oil | 350°F (177°C) | Coconut, tropical | Southeast Asian curry, baking |
| Extra virgin olive oil | 325-375°F (163-191°C) | Fruity, peppery, grassy | Finishing, vinaigrettes, low-heat saute |
| Toasted sesame oil | 350°F (177°C) | Intense, nutty | Finishing only---not a cooking oil |

## Clarified Butter and Ghee

**Clarified butter** is whole butter from which water (approximately 15-17% of whole butter) and milk solids (casein, whey proteins, lactose) have been removed, leaving pure butterfat. It is the milk solids that burn at 350°F (177°C), so their removal raises the effective smoke point to approximately 450°F (232°C).

**Method**: Melt unsalted butter over low heat without stirring. Three layers will form: foam (whey proteins) on top, clear golden butterfat in the middle, and watery milk solids (casein) at the bottom. Skim the foam, then carefully ladle or pour the clear butterfat through cheesecloth, leaving the milky residue behind. Yield: approximately 75-80% of original butter weight.

**Ghee** takes this further: the butter is simmered until the milk solids on the bottom caramelize (turning from white to golden-brown), imparting a nuttier, toasted flavor before straining. Ghee has a slightly higher smoke point and a longer shelf life than clarified butter due to the complete removal of moisture.

## Fat Selection Guidelines

### High-Heat Cooking (Searing, Deep Frying, Wok)
Use fats with smoke points above 400°F (204°C) and neutral or complementary flavors. Refined avocado oil, peanut oil, rice bran oil, and ghee are the primary choices. For deep frying, stability over extended use matters---peanut oil and rice bran oil maintain integrity through multiple frying cycles better than most alternatives.

### Medium-Heat Cooking (Saute, Pan Frying)
Whole butter, olive oil, and lard perform well at moderate temperatures (300-375°F / 149-191°C). A combination of butter and neutral oil (50/50) is a classic French technique---the oil raises the overall smoke point while the butter contributes flavor.

### Finishing and Raw Applications
Extra virgin olive oil, toasted sesame oil, nut oils (walnut, hazelnut, pistachio), and high-quality whole butter are used unheated or added at the end of cooking to preserve their volatile flavor compounds. These fats are too costly and too aromatic to use as cooking mediums.

### Pastry Applications
Butter (82-84% fat content, European-style) is the standard for laminated doughs, pie crusts, and enriched breads. Leaf lard (rendered from the kidney area of the pig, with a clean, non-porky flavor) produces exceptionally flaky pie crusts and is preferred by many pastry professionals for American-style pies. Shortening (hydrogenated vegetable fat) has a 100% fat content (no water) and produces extremely tender baked goods but lacks flavor.

## Fatty Acid Composition Notes

- **Saturated fats** (butter, coconut oil, lard): Solid at room temperature, highly stable under heat, resistant to oxidation. Best for high-heat and extended cooking.
- **Monounsaturated fats** (olive oil, avocado oil): Liquid at room temperature, moderately stable. Good all-purpose cooking fats.
- **Polyunsaturated fats** (walnut oil, flaxseed oil, grapeseed oil): Least heat-stable, prone to oxidation. Best used cold or at low temperatures. Store refrigerated to prevent rancidity.
