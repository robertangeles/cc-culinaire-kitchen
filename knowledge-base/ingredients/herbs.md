# Culinary Herbs

## Overview

Herbs are the leaves, stems, and flowers of aromatic plants used to flavor food. Professional herb use requires understanding volatile oil chemistry, heat sensitivity, and classic pairing logic. The fundamental distinction is between tender (volatile) herbs and hardy (robust) herbs---this classification dictates when and how each is added during cooking.

## Volatile (Tender) Herbs

These herbs contain delicate essential oils that dissipate rapidly under heat. Add at the end of cooking or use raw.

### Basil
- **Key compounds**: Linalool, eugenol, estragole
- **Heat sensitivity**: High. Loses aroma within 2-3 minutes of heat exposure. Add after cooking or as a finishing garnish.
- **Classic pairings**: Tomato, mozzarella, garlic, olive oil, stone fruit, strawberry
- **Varieties**: Sweet (Genovese), Thai (anise-forward), opal (milder, decorative), lemon basil
- **Note**: Never cut basil with a dull knife---bruised cells oxidize rapidly, turning black. Chiffonade with a sharp blade or tear by hand.

### Cilantro (Coriander Leaf)
- **Key compounds**: Linalool, decenal (aldehydes responsible for the "soapy" perception in some individuals due to OR6A2 gene variation)
- **Heat sensitivity**: Very high. Add at service.
- **Classic pairings**: Lime, chili, cumin, avocado, fish, Southeast Asian and Latin cuisines
- **Stems**: Flavorful and tender---use finely minced in salsas, marinades, and curry pastes. Do not discard.

### Chervil
- **Key compounds**: Estragole, methyl chavicol
- **Heat sensitivity**: Extremely high. Used exclusively raw or as a last-second garnish.
- **Classic pairings**: Eggs, cream sauces, spring vegetables, fines herbes blend
- **Note**: One of the fines herbes (with parsley, tarragon, and chives). Essential in classical French garnish.

### Tarragon
- **Key compounds**: Estragole (dominant anise note)
- **Heat sensitivity**: Moderate. Can withstand brief cooking (final 5 minutes) but best added late.
- **Classic pairings**: Chicken, fish, eggs, bearnaise, Dijon mustard, cream, white wine vinegar
- **Note**: French tarragon only (Artemisia dracunculus var. sativa). Russian tarragon is nearly flavorless.

### Chives
- **Key compounds**: Diallyl sulfides (mild allium)
- **Heat sensitivity**: High. Always add raw.
- **Classic pairings**: Sour cream, potatoes, eggs, smoked salmon, cream cheese

### Mint
- **Key compounds**: Menthol, menthone
- **Heat sensitivity**: Moderate. Withstands brief cooking in sauces but best used fresh.
- **Classic pairings**: Lamb, peas, yogurt, chocolate, fruit desserts, Middle Eastern grain salads

## Hardy (Robust) Herbs

These herbs contain stable essential oils bound in tougher cellular structures. They withstand prolonged cooking and benefit from heat to release their flavors.

### Thyme
- **Key compounds**: Thymol, carvacrol
- **Add when**: Early in the cooking process. Withstands hours of braising or roasting.
- **Classic pairings**: Poultry, beef, mushrooms, tomato, potatoes, bouquet garni

### Rosemary
- **Key compounds**: Camphor, 1,8-cineole, alpha-pinene
- **Add when**: Early. Very robust; use with restraint as camphor can dominate.
- **Classic pairings**: Lamb, pork, potatoes, focaccia, white beans, garlic
- **Note**: Mince finely---the needle-like leaves are tough and unpleasant in large pieces.

### Sage
- **Key compounds**: Thujone, camphor, 1,8-cineole
- **Add when**: Early to mid-cooking. Excellent fried in brown butter (30-45 seconds until crisp).
- **Classic pairings**: Pork, poultry (especially turkey), brown butter, gnocchi, winter squash, sausage

### Bay Laurel
- **Key compounds**: 1,8-cineole, linalool
- **Add when**: Beginning of cooking. Requires 30+ minutes to release flavor. Remove before service.
- **Classic pairings**: Stocks, braises, bechamel, court-bouillon, tomato sauce
- **Note**: Fresh bay is significantly more aromatic than dried. Use 1 fresh leaf where a recipe calls for 2 dried.

### Oregano
- **Key compounds**: Carvacrol, thymol
- **Add when**: Early to mid-cooking. Also used dried effectively---drying concentrates oils.
- **Classic pairings**: Tomato, grilled meats, pizza, Greek and Mexican cuisines

## Storage

- **Tender herbs**: Store wrapped in damp paper towels inside a loosely sealed container in the refrigerator. Basil is the exception---store at room temperature in a glass of water, as refrigeration causes blackening (chilling injury below 40°F / 4°C).
- **Hardy herbs**: Store in the refrigerator wrapped in a damp towel. Will keep 1-2 weeks.
- **Drying**: Only effective for hardy herbs. Tender herbs lose most flavor when dried.

## Bouquet Garni and Sachet

- **Bouquet garni**: Thyme sprigs, bay leaf, and parsley stems tied with butcher's twine. The classical aromatic bundle for stocks, braises, and soups.
- **Sachet d'epices**: Peppercorns, bay leaf, thyme, parsley stems, and optionally clove or garlic, wrapped in cheesecloth. Used where a bouquet garni would be impractical.
