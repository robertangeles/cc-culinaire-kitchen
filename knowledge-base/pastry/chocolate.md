# Chocolate Tempering and Ganache

## Overview

Working with chocolate at a professional level requires an understanding of cocoa butter crystallography. Cocoa butter can crystallize in six polymorphic forms (I through VI). Only Form V (beta crystals) produces the desired snap, gloss, and contraction from the mold. Tempering is the controlled process of melting, cooling, and rewarming chocolate to seed Form V crystals throughout the mass.

## Tempering Temperatures

| Chocolate Type | Melt To | Cool To | Working Temp |
|---|---|---|---|
| Dark (55-70%) | 122-131°F (50-55°C) | 80-82°F (27-28°C) | 88-90°F (31-32°C) |
| Milk | 113-122°F (45-50°C) | 78-80°F (25-27°C) | 84-86°F (29-30°C) |
| White | 104-113°F (40-45°C) | 77-78°F (25-26°C) | 82-84°F (28-29°C) |

Use couverture chocolate (minimum 31% cocoa butter content) for all tempering applications. Compound coatings containing vegetable fats do not require tempering but lack true chocolate flavor and snap.

## Tabling Method

The classical technique. Requires a clean marble or granite slab.

1. Melt chocolate to the "melt" temperature in a bain-marie or microwave at 50% power in 30-second intervals, stirring between each.
2. Pour two-thirds of the melted chocolate onto the marble surface. Reserve one-third in the bowl.
3. Work the chocolate on the marble using an offset spatula and bench scraper---spread it thin, gather it, and spread again. This agitation combined with the cool marble surface encourages Form V crystal nucleation.
4. When the tabled chocolate reaches the "cool to" temperature and begins to thicken slightly, return it to the reserved warm chocolate.
5. Stir thoroughly. The final working temperature should stabilize at the "working temp."
6. Test: Dip the corner of a parchment strip. Properly tempered chocolate will set within 3-5 minutes at 65-68°F (18-20°C) room temperature with an even, glossy finish and clean snap.

## Seeding Method

More practical for production environments. Uses pre-tempered chocolate (finely chopped or callets) as seed crystals.

1. Melt chocolate to the "melt" temperature.
2. Remove from heat. Add finely chopped tempered chocolate at approximately 25-30% of the total weight.
3. Stir continuously until the seed chocolate is fully melted and the mass cools to the "cool to" temperature.
4. Gently rewarm to the "working temp" if necessary, using brief heat exposure (heat gun or 5-second microwave bursts).

## Ganache

Ganache is an emulsion of chocolate and cream (and sometimes butter). The ratio of chocolate to cream determines the set and application.

| Application | Dark Chocolate : Cream | Milk Chocolate : Cream |
|---|---|---|
| Pourable glaze | 1 : 2 | 1 : 1.5 |
| Cake filling/frosting | 1 : 1 | 1.5 : 1 |
| Truffles (scoopable) | 2 : 1 | 2.5 : 1 |
| Firm ganache (slicing) | 3 : 1 | 3.5 : 1 |

**Method**: Heat cream to just below a simmer (180°F / 82°C). Pour over finely chopped chocolate. Let stand 1-2 minutes to melt. Stir from the center outward in small circles, gradually widening, to form a smooth emulsion. Do not whisk vigorously---incorporating air creates a matte finish and grainy texture. For additional richness, add soft butter (10-15% of chocolate weight) once the ganache cools to 95°F (35°C).

## Bloom Troubleshooting

- **Fat bloom**: White, chalky streaks or spots on the surface. Caused by cocoa butter migrating to the surface due to improper tempering, temperature fluctuation during storage, or age. The chocolate is safe to eat but visually compromised. Prevention: temper correctly and store at 60-65°F (15-18°C) with low humidity (below 50% RH).
- **Sugar bloom**: Grainy, white crystalline coating. Caused by moisture condensation on the surface dissolving sugar, which recrystallizes upon evaporation. Prevention: never refrigerate finished chocolate in unsealed containers. If refrigeration is necessary, wrap airtight and allow to return to room temperature before unwrapping to prevent condensation.

## Storage

Store chocolate in a cool, dry environment: 60-65°F (15-18°C), below 50% relative humidity, away from light and strong odors (cocoa butter readily absorbs volatile compounds). Properly stored couverture maintains quality for 12-18 months (dark) or 8-12 months (milk and white).
