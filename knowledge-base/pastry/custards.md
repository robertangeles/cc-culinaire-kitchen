# Custards

## Overview

Custards are egg-based preparations in which proteins coagulate to create a thickened, smooth texture. They are divided into two categories: stirred custards (creme anglaise, pastry cream) cooked on the stovetop, and baked custards (creme brulee, creme caramel, pot de creme) set in the oven. Mastery of custards requires precise temperature control and an understanding of egg protein behavior.

## Egg Protein Science

Egg yolks begin to thicken at approximately 149°F (65°C) and set firmly at 158-165°F (70-74°C). Whole eggs coagulate between 144-158°F (62-70°C). Overcooking causes the protein matrix to contract and squeeze out water---resulting in curdling (stirred) or syneresis and a porous texture (baked). Sugar raises the coagulation temperature by 5-10°F (3-6°C), providing a wider working window.

## Creme Anglaise

A stirred custard and the mother sauce of pastry. Ratio: **6 egg yolks : 1 cup (240ml) milk : 1/4 cup (50g) sugar**.

1. Combine milk with half the sugar and a split vanilla bean (or 1 tsp extract). Scald to 180°F (82°C).
2. Whisk yolks with remaining sugar until pale and slightly thickened (blanchir).
3. Temper: ladle approximately one-third of the hot milk into the yolk mixture while whisking constantly. This gradually raises the yolk temperature without curdling.
4. Return the tempered mixture to the saucepan. Cook over medium-low heat, stirring constantly with a heat-resistant spatula in a figure-eight pattern.
5. The custard is done at **170-175°F (77-80°C)**---it should coat the back of a spoon (nappant) and hold a clean line when you draw your finger across it.
6. Immediately strain through a fine-mesh sieve into a bowl set over an ice bath. Stir periodically until chilled below 40°F (4°C).

Never allow creme anglaise to exceed 180°F (82°C)---the yolks will curdle irreversibly.

## Pastry Cream (Creme Patissiere)

Ratio: **6 egg yolks : 2 cups (480ml) milk : 1/2 cup (100g) sugar : 1/4 cup (35g) cornstarch (or 1/3 cup / 45g flour)**.

Unlike creme anglaise, pastry cream is brought to a full boil. The starch must reach 203°F (95°C) to fully gelatinize and to deactivate amylase---an enzyme in egg yolks that would otherwise break down the starch and cause thinning over time.

1. Scald milk with half the sugar and vanilla.
2. Whisk yolks, remaining sugar, and starch until smooth.
3. Temper, return to heat, and whisk vigorously while bringing to a boil. Boil for 1-2 minutes, whisking constantly.
4. Remove from heat. Stir in butter (2 tbsp / 30g) for richness and sheen.
5. Pour into a shallow pan, press plastic wrap directly onto the surface (to prevent a skin), and chill rapidly.

Derivatives: Creme mousseline (pastry cream + whipped butter), creme diplomate (pastry cream + whipped cream), creme chiboust (pastry cream + Italian meringue).

## Creme Brulee

Ratio: **5 egg yolks : 2 cups (480ml) heavy cream : 1/3 cup (65g) sugar : vanilla**.

1. Scald cream with sugar and vanilla.
2. Temper into yolks. Strain.
3. Pour into ramekins. Bake in a bain-marie (water bath at 170°F / 77°C) at 300-325°F (149-163°C) for 40-50 minutes. The custard should be set at the edges but jiggle in a 2-inch circle at the center.
4. Chill at least 4 hours, preferably overnight.
5. To serve: coat surface evenly with a thin layer of superfine sugar (approximately 1 tsp per ramekin). Torch with a propane or butane torch, keeping the flame moving, until the sugar caramelizes to deep amber.

## Tempering Technique

Tempering is the controlled gradual raising of egg temperature. Add hot liquid to eggs in a thin, steady stream while whisking constantly. A ratio of approximately one-third hot liquid to eggs is standard. This raises egg temperature by 30-40°F (17-22°C), preventing the thermal shock that causes curdling. After tempering, the mixture can safely be added back to the remaining hot liquid.
