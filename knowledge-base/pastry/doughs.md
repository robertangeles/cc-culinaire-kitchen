# Pastry Doughs

## Overview

Pastry doughs are categorized by their fat incorporation method, gluten development, and resulting texture. The three foundational tart doughs (brisee, sucree, sablee), choux paste, and puff pastry form the backbone of classical pastry production. Each demands specific technique and precise ratios.

## Pate Brisee (Short Crust)

A flaky, savory dough used for quiche, tarts, and pies. Fat is cut into flour, leaving visible pieces that create flaky layers during baking.

**Ratio**: 2 parts flour : 1 part butter : cold water as needed (approximately 1/4 part)

**Formula (standard batch)**: 250g all-purpose flour, 125g cold unsalted butter (cubed), 1 tsp salt, 60-75ml ice water.

**Method**: Combine flour and salt. Cut butter into flour using a pastry cutter, bench scraper, or fingertips until the mixture resembles coarse meal with pea-sized butter pieces. Add ice water gradually, mixing just until the dough comes together. Do not overwork---visible butter streaks are desirable. Wrap in plastic, refrigerate minimum 1 hour before rolling. Fraisage (smearing with the heel of the hand) may be used to blend without overdeveloping gluten.

## Pate Sucree (Sweet Tart Dough)

A tender, cookie-like dough for dessert tarts. Fat and sugar are creamed together before flour is added---this coats flour proteins in fat, minimizing gluten development.

**Ratio**: 3 parts flour : 2 parts butter : 1 part powdered sugar : 1 egg

**Formula**: 300g flour, 200g softened butter, 100g powdered sugar, 1 whole egg, pinch of salt, vanilla.

**Method (creaming)**: Cream butter and sugar until pale. Add egg and vanilla. Incorporate flour and salt on low speed just until combined. The dough will be soft. Wrap and chill at least 2 hours. Roll between parchment sheets at 1/8 inch (3mm) thickness.

## Pate Sablee (Sandy Dough)

The most tender and crumbly of the three, used for petits fours, shortbread, and delicate tart shells. Higher fat and sugar content than sucree.

**Ratio**: 3 parts flour : 2 parts butter : 1.5 parts powdered sugar : 1 egg yolk (per 150g flour)

**Formula**: 300g flour, 200g butter, 150g powdered sugar, 2 yolks, salt, vanilla.

**Method (sablage)**: Rub softened butter into flour until the mixture has a sandy texture (sable = sand). Incorporate sugar, then yolks. Minimal mixing. Rest and chill thoroughly---this dough is extremely fragile at room temperature.

## Pate a Choux (Choux Paste)

A cooked dough (panade) that relies on steam for leavening, not chemical leaveners. The high moisture content converts to steam in a hot oven, inflating the paste.

**Ratio**: 1 part water : 1 part milk : 1 part butter : 1.2 parts flour : 4 eggs (per 125g flour)

**Formula**: 125ml water, 125ml whole milk, 113g butter, 5g salt, 8g sugar, 150g bread flour, 4-5 large eggs.

**Method**:
1. Bring water, milk, butter, salt, and sugar to a rolling boil. All butter must be melted before the boil.
2. Add flour all at once. Stir vigorously with a wooden spoon over medium heat until the dough forms a ball and a thin film coats the bottom of the pan (this is called dessecher---drying out). Cook 2-3 minutes.
3. Transfer to a stand mixer with the paddle attachment. Mix on medium speed to cool slightly (below 140°F / 60°C).
4. Add eggs one at a time, fully incorporating each before adding the next. The final paste should be glossy and fall from a spatula in a thick V-shape.
5. Pipe and bake: 400°F (204°C) for 15 minutes (initial steam burst), then reduce to 350°F (177°C) for 20-25 minutes. Do not open the oven during the first 20 minutes.

## Puff Pastry (Pate Feuilletee)

A laminated dough with no chemical leavener. Layers of butter trapped between layers of dough create steam pockets, yielding up to 729 distinct layers after six turns.

**Ratio**: Equal parts flour and butter by weight (e.g., 500g flour : 500g butter). The detrempe (dough block) contains approximately 50% of the liquid needed.

**Method**:
1. Make the detrempe: 500g flour, 250ml cold water, 10g salt, 50g melted butter. Mix until smooth but not elastic. Rest 30 minutes.
2. Pound the butter block (beurre de tourage) into a 7-inch square, keeping it cold but pliable (60-65°F / 15-18°C).
3. Encase butter in detrempe (envelope method).
4. Roll and fold: standard single (letter) fold = 3 layers. Perform 6 single folds with 30-minute rests in refrigeration between every 2 turns. Alternatively, 3 double (book) folds may be used.
5. Final rest of at least 1 hour before cutting and baking at 400-425°F (204-218°C).
