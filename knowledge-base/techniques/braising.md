# Braising

## Overview

Braising is a combination cooking method that uses dry heat (searing) followed by moist heat (cooking in a covered vessel with liquid) to convert tough, collagen-rich cuts into tender, deeply flavored dishes. It is the definitive technique for secondary and tertiary cuts---shanks, shoulders, cheeks, and short ribs.

## Collagen Conversion

The science of braising centers on the hydrolysis of collagen into gelatin. Collagen, the primary structural protein in connective tissue, begins to denature at approximately 160°F (71°C) and converts to gelatin most efficiently between 180-205°F (82-96°C). This process requires both heat and time---above 205°F (96°C), muscle fibers contract excessively and squeeze out moisture, resulting in dry, stringy meat despite tender connective tissue.

Target internal braising liquid temperature: **185-200°F (85-93°C)**---a gentle, barely perceptible simmer. A rolling boil (212°F / 100°C) is too aggressive and will toughen proteins.

The conversion is not instant. Collagen hydrolysis follows time-dependent kinetics. Even at optimal temperatures, tough cuts require extended cooking for complete conversion. The gelatin produced enriches the braising liquid with body and viscosity, creating a sauce that coats the palate.

## Liquid Ratios

Braising is not stewing. The protein should be partially submerged---typically one-half to two-thirds covered by liquid.

- **Braise**: Liquid covers 1/2 to 2/3 of the protein. Exposed surfaces develop concentration of flavor.
- **Stew**: Protein is fully submerged, cut into smaller pieces (1-2 inch cubes). Higher liquid ratio.
- **Pot roast**: Large single piece, liquid at 1/3 to 1/2 depth.

Standard braising liquid components: stock, wine, tomato product, and aromatics. A typical ratio for a 3-4 lb braise: **2-3 cups stock, 1 cup wine, mirepoix (1 part each), bouquet garni**.

## Vessel Selection

- **Enameled cast iron (e.g., Le Creuset, Staub)**: The gold standard. Heavy mass provides even heat distribution. Enamel interior allows fond development without reactivity with acidic liquids. Tight-fitting lids minimize evaporation.
- **Heavy stainless steel**: Acceptable. May develop hot spots on thinner-bottomed models.
- **Raw cast iron**: Reactive with acidic braising liquids (wine, tomatoes). Can impart metallic off-flavors in braises exceeding 2 hours.
- **Clay/ceramic**: Excellent heat diffusion. Requires careful temperature management to avoid thermal shock.

The vessel should be sized to fit the protein snugly. Excess headspace means excess liquid, which dilutes flavor.

## Timing by Protein

All times assume oven braising at 300-325°F (149-163°C):

| Protein | Cut | Time |
|---|---|---|
| Beef short ribs | Bone-in, 2-3 inch pieces | 3-3.5 hours |
| Beef chuck | 3-4 lb piece | 3-4 hours |
| Beef cheeks | Whole, trimmed | 3-3.5 hours |
| Lamb shanks | Whole, frenched | 2.5-3 hours |
| Lamb shoulder | Bone-in, 3-4 lb | 3-4 hours |
| Pork shoulder | 3-5 lb piece | 3.5-4.5 hours |
| Pork belly | 2-3 lb slab | 2.5-3 hours |
| Chicken thighs | Bone-in, skin-on | 1.5-2 hours |
| Veal osso buco | Cross-cut shank, 1.5 inch | 2-2.5 hours |
| Oxtail | Cut at joints | 3.5-4 hours |

## Best Practices

1. **Sear thoroughly** before braising. Develop deep color on all sides---this is not about "sealing in juices" (a myth) but about Maillard reaction flavor development.
2. **Deglaze** the searing pan to capture the fond.
3. **Braise in the oven**, not on the stovetop. Oven heat surrounds the vessel evenly, whereas stovetop heat is directional and creates hot spots.
4. **Rest overnight** when possible. Cool the braise, refrigerate, and reheat the next day. Gelatin re-absorbs into the meat fibers, fat solidifies on the surface for easy removal, and flavors meld.
