# Emulsions

## Overview

An emulsion is a stable dispersion of two immiscible liquids---typically fat and water---achieved through mechanical energy and emulsifying agents. Mastery of emulsions is foundational to classical French technique and underpins sauces from hollandaise to beurre blanc to vinaigrettes.

## Emulsion Science

Emulsions are classified as either oil-in-water (O/W) or water-in-oil (W/O). Most culinary emulsions are O/W: fat droplets suspended in a continuous aqueous phase. Stability depends on three factors:

- **Emulsifier**: Molecules with both hydrophilic and hydrophobic regions (amphiphilic) that position themselves at the oil-water interface. Egg yolk lecithin (phosphatidylcholine) is the classic culinary emulsifier. Mustard mucilage, casein in dairy, and soy lecithin also function effectively.
- **Droplet size**: Smaller droplets create more stable emulsions. Vigorous whisking, blending, or shearing reduces droplet diameter.
- **Viscosity**: A thicker continuous phase slows droplet migration and coalescence.

## Hollandaise

A warm emulsified sauce. Base ratio: **3 egg yolks : 8 oz (225g) clarified butter : 1 tbsp water or reduction**.

1. Combine yolks and water (or a white wine/vinegar reduction---2 tbsp wine, 1 tbsp vinegar, reduced to 1 tbsp) in a bain-marie.
2. Whisk constantly over simmering water (140-150°F / 60-66°C at the bowl). The yolks must thicken to the ribbon stage (sabayon) before adding fat---this is where the lecithin activates as an emulsifier through protein denaturation.
3. Remove from heat. Add clarified butter in a thin, steady stream while whisking. The emulsion will thicken progressively.
4. Season with lemon juice, cayenne, and salt. Hold between 135-145°F (57-63°C). Above 160°F (71°C), the yolk proteins will coagulate and the sauce breaks.

## Vinaigrettes

Standard ratio: **3 parts oil : 1 part acid**. This is a temporary emulsion unless stabilized.

- **Temporary**: Whisk oil into acid. Will separate within minutes.
- **Semi-stable**: Add Dijon mustard (1 tsp per cup of vinaigrette). Mustard mucilage and particulate matter stabilize the emulsion for hours.
- **Stable**: Add egg yolk or soy lecithin (0.5-1% by weight of total liquid). Creates emulsions stable for days under refrigeration.

## Beurre Blanc

A water-in-fat emulsion unique in its fragility. Ratio: **2 tbsp shallots : 1/2 cup white wine : 2 tbsp white wine vinegar : 8 oz (225g) cold whole butter**.

1. Sweat shallots, add wine and vinegar, reduce to approximately 2 tablespoons (au sec).
2. Over low heat (not exceeding 160°F / 71°C), whisk in cold butter cut into 1-tablespoon pieces, one at a time.
3. The casein and whey proteins in whole butter act as emulsifiers. The butterfat melts into the aqueous reduction while milk solids stabilize the suspension.
4. Strain through a fine-mesh sieve. Hold at 135-145°F (57-63°C). Temperatures above 160°F (71°C) will cause the emulsion to separate into clarified butter and water.

## Recovering Broken Emulsions

- **Hollandaise**: Start a new sabayon with one yolk and 1 tablespoon water. Slowly whisk the broken sauce into the fresh sabayon.
- **Beurre blanc**: Remove from heat. Add 1 tablespoon ice water or cold cream and whisk vigorously. The cold liquid provides new aqueous phase and drops the temperature.
- **Vinaigrette**: Place 1 teaspoon mustard in a clean bowl. Slowly re-whisk the broken vinaigrette into the mustard.

## Lecithin Applications

Soy lecithin (available as powder or granules) enables foams and ultra-light emulsions. Use at 0.5-1.0% by weight of liquid. Disperse with an immersion blender. Lecithin foams are stable for 30-60 minutes and offer texture without added fat.
