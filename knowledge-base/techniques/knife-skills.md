# Classical Knife Cuts

## Overview

Precision knife cuts are foundational to professional cookery. Uniform cuts ensure even cooking, consistent texture, and refined presentation. The classical French repertoire defines specific dimensions for each cut, and professional kitchens hold cooks to these standards during mise en place.

## Standard Cuts and Dimensions

### Brunoise
- **Dimensions**: 1/8 inch x 1/8 inch x 1/8 inch (3mm x 3mm x 3mm)
- **Method**: First cut julienne, then cross-cut at 1/8-inch intervals.
- **Applications**: Garnish for consomme, fine sauces, tartare accompaniments, delicate vegetable garnish.

### Fine Brunoise
- **Dimensions**: 1/16 inch x 1/16 inch x 1/16 inch (1.5mm x 1.5mm x 1.5mm)
- **Method**: Cut fine julienne, then cross-cut at 1/16-inch intervals.
- **Applications**: Royal garnish, consomme brunoise, sauce accents.

### Julienne
- **Dimensions**: 1/8 inch x 1/8 inch x 2-2.5 inches (3mm x 3mm x 5-6cm)
- **Method**: Square off the vegetable, cut into 1/8-inch planks, stack planks and cut into 1/8-inch strips.
- **Applications**: Stir-fry, salads, garnish, crudite.

### Fine Julienne (Allumette)
- **Dimensions**: 1/16 inch x 1/16 inch x 2-2.5 inches (1.5mm x 1.5mm x 5-6cm)
- **Applications**: Delicate garnish, pommes allumettes (matchstick fries).

### Batonnet
- **Dimensions**: 1/4 inch x 1/4 inch x 2-2.5 inches (6mm x 6mm x 5-6cm)
- **Method**: Square off the vegetable, cut 1/4-inch planks, stack and cut into 1/4-inch strips.
- **Applications**: Crudite, pommes frites, vegetable sticks for glazing.

### Small Dice (Macédoine)
- **Dimensions**: 1/4 inch x 1/4 inch x 1/4 inch (6mm x 6mm x 6mm)
- **Method**: Cross-cut batonnet at 1/4-inch intervals.
- **Applications**: Salsas, vegetable garnish, macédoine of vegetables.

### Medium Dice (Parmentier)
- **Dimensions**: 1/2 inch x 1/2 inch x 1/2 inch (12mm x 12mm x 12mm)
- **Applications**: Soups, stews, hash, home-fry potatoes.

### Large Dice (Carre)
- **Dimensions**: 3/4 inch x 3/4 inch x 3/4 inch (2cm x 2cm x 2cm)
- **Applications**: Rustic stews, roasted vegetable preparations, stocks.

### Chiffonade
- **Dimensions**: 1/16 to 1/8 inch (1.5-3mm) ribbons
- **Method**: Stack leaves (basil, sorrel, lettuce, etc.), roll tightly into a cigar shape, slice perpendicular to the roll.
- **Applications**: Garnish for soups, pastas, and plated dishes. Cut immediately before service---leafy herbs oxidize rapidly.

### Paysanne
- **Dimensions**: 1/2 inch x 1/2 inch x 1/8 inch (12mm x 12mm x 3mm) thin flat pieces
- **Shape**: Follows the natural shape of the vegetable (rounds, half-moons, triangles, squares).
- **Applications**: Soups, particularly potage paysanne.

### Tourner (Turned Vegetables)
- **Dimensions**: Approximately 2 inches (5cm) long, seven-sided football shape
- **Method**: Using a tourner or paring knife, carve the vegetable into an oblong barrel with seven equal faces. Requires significant practice.
- **Applications**: Classical vegetable garnitures---bouquetiere, jardiniere. A benchmark of knife proficiency.

## Mirepoix

Not a single cut but a preparation: **2 parts onion : 1 part carrot : 1 part celery** by weight. Standard mirepoix is cut in 1/2-inch (12mm) dice for long-cooking applications (stocks, braises). For shorter-cooking applications, cut smaller---1/4-inch dice. White mirepoix (onion, celery, leek, parsnip---no carrot) is used for white stocks and cream-based sauces where carrot color is undesirable.

## Knife Selection

- **Chef's knife (8-10 inch)**: Primary workhorse for all major cuts.
- **Paring knife (3-4 inch)**: Tourner, small detail work, peeling.
- **Nakiri or usuba**: Ideal for precision vegetable work; flat blade ensures full contact with the cutting board.

Maintain a 15-20 degree edge angle. Hone before every service on a ceramic or steel honing rod. Sharpen on whetstones (1000/3000/6000 grit progression) as needed.
