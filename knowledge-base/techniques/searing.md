# Pan Searing Proteins

## Overview

Pan searing is a dry-heat cooking method that develops complex flavors through the Maillard reaction---a non-enzymatic browning reaction between amino acids and reducing sugars that begins at approximately 280°F (138°C) and accelerates between 310-330°F (154-166°C). Mastery of searing requires precise heat control, proper protein preparation, and an understanding of fat selection.

## The Maillard Reaction

The Maillard reaction produces hundreds of distinct flavor compounds. It is not caramelization, which involves only sugars at higher temperatures (320°F/160°C and above). Key factors influencing the reaction:

- **Surface moisture**: The single greatest impediment to searing. Surface water must evaporate (absorbing energy at 212°F/100°C) before the pan surface can reach Maillard temperatures. Pat proteins thoroughly dry and, where appropriate, air-dry uncovered in refrigeration for 12-24 hours.
- **pH**: Alkaline environments accelerate the reaction. A light dusting of baking soda (approximately 1/8 tsp per pound of meat, applied 15-20 minutes before cooking) raises surface pH and promotes faster browning.
- **Sugar availability**: Proteins with higher free amino acid and sugar content brown more readily. Dry-aged beef sears faster than wet-aged due to enzymatic breakdown increasing available reactants.

## Oil Selection

Choose fats by smoke point relative to your target searing temperature:

| Fat | Smoke Point | Best Use |
|---|---|---|
| Refined avocado oil | 520°F (271°C) | High-heat searing |
| Refined safflower oil | 510°F (266°C) | High-heat searing |
| Clarified butter/ghee | 450°F (232°C) | Medium-high searing with butter flavor |
| Whole butter | 350°F (177°C) | Finishing/basting only |
| Extra virgin olive oil | 325-375°F (163-191°C) | Not recommended for searing |

Use a thin film of oil (approximately 1-2 tablespoons per 12-inch skillet). The oil acts as a heat-transfer medium, filling micro-irregularities in the pan surface.

## Scallop Technique

Dry-packed (untreated) U-10 sea scallops are the standard. Avoid STP-treated scallops---they retain excess water and will not sear properly.

1. Remove the side muscle (adductor). Pat scallops dry, then rest on paper towels uncovered in refrigeration for 30-60 minutes.
2. Season with fine salt immediately before cooking---salt draws moisture if applied too early.
3. Heat a carbon steel or stainless-steel pan over high heat until oil shimmers and just begins to smoke.
4. Place scallops clockwise, leaving at least 1 inch between each. Overcrowding drops pan temperature and causes steaming.
5. Do not move for 2-2.5 minutes. A deep golden-brown crust should release naturally from the pan. Flip once. Cook an additional 1.5-2 minutes for a warm, translucent center (internal temperature 115-120°F / 46-49°C).

## Fond Development

Fond---the caramelized protein and sugar residue adhering to the pan---is the foundation of pan sauces. After searing, deglaze with wine, stock, or other liquid while the pan is still hot. Residual heat between 300-350°F (149-177°C) is ideal; if the fond turns black, the pan was too hot, and the fond will taste acrid and bitter. A properly developed fond should be deep amber, not charcoal.

## Pan Selection

Heavy-bottomed stainless steel, carbon steel, or cast iron are preferred. Non-stick coatings prevent fond formation and cannot withstand the temperatures required. Carbon steel offers the best combination of heat responsiveness and searing capability.
